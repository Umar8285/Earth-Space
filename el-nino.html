Understanding El Niño

What is El Niño?

El Niño is a natural climatic phenomenon that happens whenever the Pacific Ocean along the equator warms up. It influences the climate anywhere on the planet. It normally happens every 2 to 7 years and lasts for months.

Pacific Ocean temperature map showing El Niño

During El Niño, the central and east Pacific become unusualy warm.

How El Niño Forms

  • Normally, warm water is pushed westward by the winds across the Pacific
  • These winds relax or reverse direction during El Niño and warm water spreads out to the east.
  • The shift of sea temperature also changes the air pressure and wind patterns over the sea

World Weather and Climate Impacts

  • North and South America: More rain, storms, and even floods in areas like California and Peru.
  • Southeast Asia and Australia:Warmer and more arid conditions, occasionally equivalent to drought and bush fires.
  • Global temperatures: Average global temperatures are higher in El Niño years, which result in climate extremes.

Dry, parched land due to drought Australia tends to experience drought and bush fires during El Niño years.

Effects on Oceans and Ecosystems

El Niño also harms sea animals. When warm water stays on the surface, it blocks cold water that is full of nutrients from coming up. That harms fish and other sea animals that depend on that for their nourishment. Coral reefs also bleach and die from the extra heat.

How Scientists Track El Niño

Scientists monitor ocean temperatures from satellites and buoys. Computer models are also employed to predict the intensity of the next El Niño and how soon it will happen. This helps people to prepare ahead of time for extreme weather.
